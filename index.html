Steve Jobs

Steve Jobs (February 24, 1955 – October 5, 2011) was an American businessman, inventor, and investor. He is best known for co-founding the technology giant Apple Inc. Jobs also founded NeXT and served as chairman and majority shareholder of Pixar.

Achievements

Early Life and Co-Founding Apple:

Born in San Francisco, Jobs was adopted shortly after birth. In 1976, he co-founded Apple with Steve Wozniak to develop and sell the Apple I personal computer. The duo gained fame and wealth with the production and sale of the Apple II, one of the first highly successful mass-produced microcomputers.

Innovations and Impact:

Jobs recognized the commercial potential of the Xerox Alto in 1979, leading to the development of the Apple Lisa (1983) and the breakthrough Macintosh (1984), the first mass-produced computer with a graphical user interface (GUI). The Macintosh revolutionized the industry and launched desktop publishing with the addition of the Apple LaserWriter, the first laser printer with vector graphics and PostScript. His vision and leadership shaped Apple’s success and its iconic products, including the iPod, iPhone, and iPad.

Departure from Apple and NeXT:

In 1985, Jobs left Apple after a power struggle with the company’s board and CEO. He founded NeXT, a computer platform development company focused on higher education and business markets.

Pixar and Legacy:

Jobs played a crucial role in developing the visual effects industry by funding the computer graphics division of Lucasfilm, which later became Pixar. Pixar produced the first 3D computer-animated feature film, Toy Story (1995), and became a leading animation studio.

Personal Life and Honors:

Jobs married Laurene Powell in 1991 and had four children. His impact on technology, design, and business earned him numerous awards, including the Presidential Medal of Freedom (posthumous, 2022)
